# Greek History: A Comprehensive Overview

## 1. Prehistoric Greece (Before 800 BCE)

### Cycladic Civilization (3200–1050 BCE)
The Cycladic culture flourished on the islands of the Aegean Sea. Known for their distinctive marble figurines, the Cycladic people were skilled seafarers and traders. Their economy was based on fishing, agriculture, and commerce.

### Minoan Civilization (2700–1450 BCE)
Centered on the island of Crete, the Minoans were one of the first advanced civilizations in Europe.
- **Capital:** Knossos
- **Key Features:** Palace complexes, Linear A script (still undeciphered), vibrant frescoes, and a sophisticated bureaucracy
- **Trade:** Extensive maritime trade with Egypt, the Levant, and mainland Greece
- **Decline:** Likely caused by the Thera (Santorini) volcanic eruption (~1600 BCE) and subsequent Mycenaean invasions

### Mycenaean Civilization (1600–1100 BCE)
The Mycenaeans were the first Greek-speaking civilization on the mainland.
- **Key Cities:** Mycenae, Tiryns, Pylos, Athens
- **Script:** Linear B (early form of Greek)
- **Culture:** Warrior aristocracy, massive stone fortifications (Cyclopean walls)
- **Notable Events:** The legendary Trojan War (~1180 BCE), described in Homer's *Iliad*
- **Collapse:** The Bronze Age Collapse (~1200–1150 BCE) brought the end of Mycenaean culture, triggering the Greek Dark Ages

---

## 2. The Greek Dark Ages (1100–800 BCE)

Following the Bronze Age Collapse, Greece experienced:
- Significant population decline
- Loss of literacy and trade networks
- Fragmentation into isolated communities
- Gradual emergence of the **polis** (city-state) model
- Oral tradition preserving myths and history (Homer composed the *Iliad* and *Odyssey* during this era)

---

## 3. The Archaic Period (800–480 BCE)

### Rise of the Polis (City-States)
City-states became the dominant political unit. Each polis had its own government, laws, army, and identity. Major poleis included:
- **Athens** – democracy and intellectual culture
- **Sparta** – military oligarchy
- **Corinth** – trade and commerce
- **Thebes** – military power in central Greece

### Greek Colonization
Greeks established colonies throughout the Mediterranean and Black Sea regions, including:
- Southern Italy and Sicily (*Magna Graecia*)
- North Africa (e.g., Cyrene)
- The Black Sea coast

### Invention of the Greek Alphabet
Around 800 BCE, the Greeks adapted the Phoenician alphabet, creating the basis for modern Western writing systems.

### Archaic Art and Philosophy
- Development of monumental sculpture (*kouroi* and *korai*)
- Birth of Greek lyric poetry (Sappho, Pindar)
- Early philosophical thought: Thales, Anaximander, and the Pre-Socratics

### Political Reforms
- **Draco (621 BCE):** Athens' first written law code
- **Solon (594 BCE):** Reforms reducing debt slavery and expanding political participation
- **Cleisthenes (508 BCE):** Laid the groundwork for Athenian democracy

### Greco-Persian Wars – First Phase
- **Battle of Marathon (490 BCE):** Athens repelled the first Persian invasion under Darius I

---

## 4. The Classical Period (480–323 BCE)

### Greco-Persian Wars – Second Phase
- **Thermopylae (480 BCE):** King Leonidas and 300 Spartans held off the Persian army of Xerxes
- **Salamis (480 BCE):** The Greek naval victory that turned the tide
- **Plataea (479 BCE):** Final Persian defeat on Greek soil

### The Athenian Golden Age (461–429 BCE)
Under **Pericles**, Athens reached its cultural and political peak:
- Completion of the **Parthenon** on the Acropolis (447–432 BCE)
- Flourishing of **tragedy** (Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides) and **comedy** (Aristophanes)
- **Philosophy:** Socrates challenged conventional wisdom; Plato and Aristotle built enduring philosophical systems
- **Herodotus** wrote the first historical accounts; **Thucydides** pioneered critical historiography

### Athenian Democracy
- **Ekklesia:** Open assembly of male citizens
- **Boule:** Council of 500 managing day-to-day governance
- **Courts:** Large juries of citizens decided legal cases
- **Ostracism:** Mechanism to exile potentially dangerous politicians

### The Peloponnesian War (431–404 BCE)
A devastating conflict between Athens and Sparta (and their respective allies).
- Caused by growing Athenian power and Spartan fear
- **Phase 1 – Archidamian War (431–421 BCE):** Inconclusive fighting; ended with Peace of Nicias
- **Sicilian Expedition (415–413 BCE):** Catastrophic Athenian defeat
- **Phase 2 (413–404 BCE):** Sparta, aided by Persia, defeats Athens
- **Result:** Spartan hegemony; Athens' empire dismantled

### Spartan and Theban Hegemony (404–338 BCE)
- Spartan dominance provoked resistance and eventually defeat at the **Battle of Leuctra (371 BCE)** by Thebes
- **Epaminondas** of Thebes introduced new military tactics (oblique order)
- Theban hegemony was short-lived after Epaminondas' death (362 BCE)

### Rise of Macedon
**Philip II of Macedon** modernized his army with the **Macedonian phalanx** and subdued the Greek city-states.
- **Battle of Chaeronea (338 BCE):** Philip defeated the combined forces of Athens and Thebes, ending Greek independence

---

## 5. The Hellenistic Period (323–31 BCE)

### Alexander the Great (336–323 BCE)
Son of Philip II, Alexander became one of history's greatest military commanders.
- Conquered the **Persian Empire**, Egypt, Bactria, and reached the Indian subcontinent
- Founded over 70 cities, most notably **Alexandria** in Egypt
- Spread Greek language and culture (*Hellenism*) across a vast territory
- Died in Babylon in 323 BCE at age 32, leaving no clear successor

### The Diadochi (Successor Kingdoms)
After Alexander's death, his generals (*Diadochi*) carved up his empire:
| Kingdom | Ruler | Region |
|---|---|---|
| Ptolemaic Kingdom | Ptolemy I | Egypt |
| Seleucid Empire | Seleucus I | Persia, Mesopotamia, Syria |
| Antigonid Kingdom | Antigonus / Demetrius | Macedonia, Greece |
| Kingdom of Pergamon | Attalid dynasty | Western Anatolia |

### Hellenistic Culture
- **Library of Alexandria:** Greatest center of learning in the ancient world
- **Science:** Archimedes, Euclid, Eratosthenes (calculated Earth's circumference)
- **Philosophy:** Stoicism (Zeno of Citium), Epicureanism (Epicurus)
- **Art:** More naturalistic and emotional sculpture (e.g., *Laocoön and His Sons*, *Venus de Milo*)

### End of the Hellenistic Age
Rome gradually absorbed the Hellenistic kingdoms:
- **Macedonia** became a Roman province in 146 BCE
- **Greece (Achaea)** became a Roman province in 146 BCE (after the sack of Corinth)
- **Egypt** (last Hellenistic kingdom) fell in **31 BCE** after the Battle of Actium and Cleopatra VII's death

## 6. Key Themes in Greek History

### Government and Politics
| System | Example | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Monarchy | Mycenaean kings | Rule by a single hereditary king |
| Oligarchy | Sparta | Rule by a small group of elites |
| Tyranny | Peisistratos of Athens | Rule by a single non-hereditary ruler |
| Democracy | Classical Athens | Rule by (male) citizens |

### Religion and Mythology
- Zeus Zeus, in ancient Greek religion, chief deity of the pantheon, a sky and weather god who was identical with the Roman god Jupiter. His name may be related to that of the sky god Dyaus
- The **Olympian gods** (Zeus, Hera, Athena, Apollo, etc.) were central to Greek identity
- **Oracles** (especially Delphi) guided political and personal decisions
- **Panhellenic festivals** (Olympic Games, Pythian Games) unified the Greek world
- Religion permeated law, warfare, agriculture, and daily life

### Philosophy and Science
- **Pre-Socratics:** Sought natural explanations for the universe (Thales, Heraclitus, Democritus)
- **Socrates (470–399 BCE):** The Socratic method; "Know thyself"
- **Plato (428–348 BCE):** Theory of Forms; *The Republic*
- **Aristotle (384–322 BCE):** Logic, biology, ethics, politics; tutor of Alexander the Great

### Military Innovations
- **Hoplite warfare:** Heavy infantry in phalanx formation
- **Trireme:** Oared warship dominating Mediterranean naval warfare
- **Macedonian phalanx:** Longer spears (*sarissa*) for greater reach and power

---

## 7. Legacy of Ancient Greece

Ancient Greece's influence on Western civilization is immense and enduring:

- **Democracy:** The concept of citizen governance originated in Athens
- **Philosophy:** Greek thinkers defined logic, ethics, and metaphysics
- **Science & Mathematics:** Foundations of geometry, physics, and astronomy
- **Literature & Drama:** Epic poetry, tragedy, and comedy shaped all later literary traditions
- **Art & Architecture:** The classical orders (Doric, Ionic, Corinthian) remain influential
- **Language:** Greek roots form a large portion of scientific and academic vocabulary
- **Olympics:** The ancient Olympic Games (started 776 BCE) inspired the modern Olympics
